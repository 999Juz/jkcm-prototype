01
East meets West — a different way of seeing

Western medicine and Chinese medicine ask fundamentally different questions. Western medicine asks: what disease is this, and how do we suppress it? Chinese medicine asks: why is this person's energy out of balance, and how do we help them restore it?

Western medicine
Treats the site of disease. Diagnoses by symptom — high blood pressure, hormonal imbalance, inflammation. Seeks external causes: viruses, bacteria, deficiencies.
Chinese medicine
Treats the energy behind disease. Diagnoses by pattern — excess heat, stagnant Qi, Yin or Yang deficiency. Looks at the whole person, not the isolated symptom.

The most memorable way to put it: the west fights the invader. The east strengthens the warrior.

When it comes to women's hormonal health, this distinction matters enormously. Two women might both present with irregular cycles and fatigue — but in Chinese medicine they may have entirely different patterns requiring entirely different treatment. One may have Liver Qi stagnation driving the irregularity, another may have underlying Blood deficiency. The symptoms look similar. The roots are different. The treatments are different.

"When a number of people with similar symptoms see a Chinese medicine practitioner, they will likely receive totally different treatments. This is Chinese medicine treating the person, not the disease."

— Dr Justin Keane

This is why Chinese medicine has served women so well across thousands of years. The female hormonal landscape is not uniform — it shifts across the reproductive years, perimenopause, menopause and beyond. A medicine that treats the individual, not the diagnosis, is exactly what this complexity requires.

02
How Chinese Medicine works

The aim of Chinese Medicine is to restore and maintain the natural order of your energy. Disease arises from disorder of the body's energies — chronic disease from longstanding disorder. Your body is always pursuing balance, working every day to achieve it. Pain and dysfunction are your body telling you where it is struggling.

The practitioner's role is to listen, and then to assist in correcting the disorder. When this happens, your body heals itself.

Chinese medicine works with several fundamental substances — here is what they mean in plain terms:

Qi (Chi) — your vital energy
The force of life. When Qi flows freely, you feel vital, warm and well. When it stagnates — as it commonly does under stress, emotional strain or hormonal fluctuation — you feel irritable, tight, blocked. Restoring Qi flow is central to women's hormonal treatment.
Blood (Xue) — nourishment and grounding
In Chinese medicine, Blood is far more than red fluid — it nourishes the body, anchors the mind and supports the menstrual cycle. Blood deficiency is one of the most common findings in women presenting with fatigue, poor sleep, pale complexion and scanty periods.
Jing — your constitutional essence
Stored in the Kidneys, Jing governs development, reproduction and ageing. The hormonal shifts of perimenopause and menopause are understood in Chinese medicine as a natural transition in Jing — not a deficiency to be corrected, but a change to be supported.
Shen — the spirit and mind
Shen governs the mind, emotions and sleep. When Shen is disturbed — often by heat, deficiency or sustained stress — anxiety, insomnia and emotional volatility follow. This is one of the most significant and underrecognised aspects of perimenopause.
Yin and Yang
The fundamental duality of the body. Yin is cooling, nourishing, fluid — Yang is warming, activating, dynamic. The hormonal changes of menopause are often understood as a shift in the Yin/Yang balance, particularly a decline in Kidney Yin that produces the characteristic heat and dryness.

Treatment works by identifying which of these substances is deficient, excessive or stuck, and then using herbal medicine to restore balance. Chinese medicine is causal — we treat the root, and symptoms disappear. Frequently, other seemingly unrelated problems resolve as well, because the root of the problem is what's being treated.

03
Herbal medicine and your hormones

Nature provides. However hot, cold, damp, dry or stagnant we become, nature offers a substance that is appropriately cooling, heating, drying, nourishing or invigorating. Chinese herbal medicine uses other products of our environment to correct the imbalances within us. It is holistic and, when correctly prescribed, highly effective.

Why herbal medicine works well for hormonal health
Hormonal health is complex, cyclical and individual. A formula prescribed in Chinese medicine is not a standardised product — it is a combination of substances chosen specifically for your pattern, your constitution and your stage of life. The formula can be adjusted as your pattern shifts, making it particularly suited to the changing terrain of perimenopause and menopause.

On this platform, your herbal formula is determined through three steps: a detailed intake questionnaire, a photograph of your tongue — one of Chinese medicine's most reliable diagnostic tools — and a short phone consultation with Dr Keane. This is the same diagnostic process used in clinic, compressed into a format accessible from anywhere in Australia.

The tongue reflects the state of your internal environment — its colour, coating, shape and moisture all carry clinical information. A yellow coat suggests heat. A pale body suggests Blood deficiency. A thick, greasy coat suggests Phlegm Damp accumulation. These findings, combined with your symptom picture, guide the formula.

Your formula is then prepared and posted to you. As your pattern shifts — which it will — the formula can be updated at your next consultation.

04
A brief history

Chinese Medicine originated thousands of years ago — not much younger than Chinese civilisation itself. The early Chinese understood that life was best approached from a perspective of health, and so the philosophy of preventative medicine became woven into the culture: in the arts, cuisine, exercise and daily life.

Shen Nong — the mythical forefather of Chinese medicine — is credited with being the first herbalist, teaching how to recognise medicinal herbs through taste and observation. Huang Ti, the Yellow Emperor, is credited with being the pioneer of Chinese medical science. His recorded conversations with his physician Chi Po became the Huang Ti Nei Jing — the Yellow Emperor's Classic — the foundational text of Chinese medicine, recorded around 300 BC.

"In nurturing life, the wise adapt to the changes of the four seasons, leading a life of equanimity, free from either over-joy or anger, adjusting Yin and Yang. In so doing, evils will rarely rise and long life obtained."

— Chi Po, from the Huang Ti Nei Jing

From as early as 1700 BC, records of remedies were kept on shells and bones. By 400 BC, complete herbal formulas were being documented. The practice of acupuncture alone is conservatively estimated to be at least 2,500 years old.

This is not an ancient system being applied to a modern problem. It is a system that has been continuously refined through clinical observation across millennia — and which has always understood the female body as something distinct, cyclical and worthy of specific attention.
