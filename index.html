Understanding Chinese Medicine

A system built on thousands of years of listening.

Chinese Medicine doesn't treat symptoms — it treats you. Here's what that means, and why it works particularly well for women's hormonal health.

In this section

01

East meets West — a different way of seeing

Western medicine and Chinese medicine ask fundamentally different questions. Western medicine asks: what disease is this, and how do we suppress it? Chinese medicine asks: why is this person's energy out of balance, and how do we help them restore it?

Western medicine
Treats the site of disease. Diagnoses by symptom. Seeks external causes: viruses, bacteria, deficiencies.
Chinese medicine
Treats the energy behind disease. Diagnoses by pattern. Looks at the whole person, not the isolated symptom.

The most memorable way to put it: the west fights the invader. The east strengthens the warrior.

"When a number of people with similar symptoms see a Chinese medicine practitioner, they will likely receive totally different treatments. This is Chinese medicine treating the person, not the disease."

— Dr Justin Keane

This is why Chinese medicine has served women so well across thousands of years. The female hormonal landscape is not uniform — it shifts across the reproductive years, perimenopause, menopause and beyond.

02

How Chinese Medicine works

The aim of Chinese Medicine is to restore and maintain the natural order of your energy. Your body is always pursuing balance, working every day to achieve it. Pain and dysfunction are your body telling you where it is struggling.

Qi (Chi) — your vital energy

The force of life. When Qi stagnates — as it commonly does under stress or hormonal fluctuation — you feel irritable, tight, blocked.

Blood (Xue) — nourishment and grounding

Nourishes the body, anchors the mind and supports the menstrual cycle. Blood deficiency is one of the most common findings in women with fatigue and poor sleep.

Jing — your constitutional essence

Stored in the Kidneys, Jing governs development, reproduction and ageing. The shifts of perimenopause and menopause are understood as a natural transition in Jing.

Shen — the spirit and mind

Governs the mind, emotions and sleep. When Shen is disturbed — often by heat or deficiency — anxiety, insomnia and emotional volatility follow.

Yin and Yang

The fundamental duality. Yin is cooling and nourishing — Yang is warming and activating. The hormonal changes of menopause often reflect a decline in Kidney Yin.

03

Herbal medicine and your hormones

Nature provides. However hot, cold, damp, dry or stagnant we become, nature offers a substance that is appropriately cooling, heating, drying, nourishing or invigorating.

Why herbal medicine works well for hormonal health

A formula prescribed in Chinese medicine is not a standardised product — it is a combination of substances chosen specifically for your pattern, your constitution and your stage of life. The formula can be adjusted as your pattern shifts.

The tongue reflects the state of your internal environment — its colour, coating, shape and moisture all carry clinical information. A yellow coat suggests heat. A pale body suggests Blood deficiency. These findings guide the formula.

04

A brief history

Chinese Medicine originated thousands of years ago. The early Chinese understood that life was best approached from a perspective of health, and the philosophy of preventative medicine became woven into the culture.

"In nurturing life, the wise adapt to the changes of the four seasons, leading a life of equanimity, free from either over-joy or anger, adjusting Yin and Yang. In so doing, evils will rarely rise and long life obtained."

— Chi Po, from the Huang Ti Nei Jing

From as early as 1700 BC, records of remedies were kept on shells and bones. The practice of acupuncture alone is conservatively estimated to be at least 2,500 years old.
